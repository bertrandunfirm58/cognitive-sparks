Natural selection acts on phenotypic variation within populations.
Mutations are the ultimate source of genetic variation.
Genetic drift has stronger effects in small populations.
Gene flow between populations reduces genetic differentiation.
Sexual selection can drive evolution of traits that reduce survival (e.g., peacock tails).
Adaptation is the process by which populations become better suited to their environment.
Convergent evolution produces similar traits in unrelated species (e.g., wings in bats and birds).
Divergent evolution leads to different traits from a common ancestor (adaptive radiation).
Coevolution occurs when two species reciprocally affect each other's evolution.
Speciation can occur through geographic isolation (allopatric) or within a population (sympatric).
The fossil record shows patterns of punctuated equilibrium — long stasis interrupted by rapid change.
Horizontal gene transfer is common in prokaryotes, complicating phylogenetic trees.
Endosymbiosis explains the origin of mitochondria and chloroplasts.
Molecular clocks use mutation rates to estimate divergence times.
Neutral theory argues most molecular evolution is driven by drift, not selection.
Evolutionary arms races between predators and prey drive rapid adaptation.
Kin selection explains altruistic behavior through inclusive fitness.
The Red Queen hypothesis suggests species must constantly evolve to maintain fitness relative to competitors.
Mass extinctions create ecological vacuums that allow surviving groups to diversify.
Epigenetic inheritance can transmit environmental effects across generations without DNA changes.
Developmental constraints limit the range of possible evolutionary outcomes.
Sexual dimorphism reflects different selection pressures on males and females.
Balancing selection maintains genetic polymorphism in populations (e.g., sickle cell trait).
