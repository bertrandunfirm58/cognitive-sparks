Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose.
The process occurs in two stages: light-dependent reactions and the Calvin cycle.
Light-dependent reactions take place in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts.
Water molecules are split, releasing oxygen as a byproduct.
The Calvin cycle occurs in the stroma and fixes CO2 into organic molecules.
RuBisCO is the most abundant enzyme on Earth and catalyzes carbon fixation.
C4 plants evolved a carbon-concentrating mechanism to reduce photorespiration.
CAM plants open stomata at night to conserve water in arid environments.
Photosystem II absorbs light at 680nm, Photosystem I at 700nm.
The Z-scheme describes electron flow through both photosystems.
ATP synthase uses the proton gradient to produce ATP (chemiosmosis).
Chlorophyll a is the primary pigment; accessory pigments expand absorption range.
Photorespiration wastes energy by fixing O2 instead of CO2.
Temperature affects enzyme activity — too high denatures RuBisCO.
Light intensity follows a saturation curve — beyond a point, more light doesn't help.
The compensation point is where photosynthesis exactly equals respiration.
Limiting factors include CO2 concentration, light intensity, and temperature.
Plants in shade develop larger leaves with more chloroplasts.
Sun plants have higher light saturation points and maximum photosynthesis rates.
Greenhouse effect increases atmospheric CO2, potentially boosting photosynthesis but also raising temperature.
Nitrogen fixation by cyanobacteria originally introduced oxygen to Earth's atmosphere.
Photosynthetic organisms produce approximately 100 billion tons of biomass annually.
Artificial photosynthesis aims to replicate the process for clean energy production.
Quantum coherence may play a role in energy transfer efficiency in photosystems.
