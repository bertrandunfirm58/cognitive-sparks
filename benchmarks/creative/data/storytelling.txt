Every story needs conflict — without it, there is no tension.
The three-act structure (setup, confrontation, resolution) underlies most narratives.
Character wants (external goal) vs needs (internal growth) drive compelling arcs.
Show, don't tell — let the reader experience through sensory detail and action.
Dialogue should reveal character, advance plot, or both — never just convey information.
Subtext: what characters don't say is often more powerful than what they do.
The inciting incident disrupts the protagonist's ordinary world.
Rising action escalates stakes through increasingly difficult obstacles.
The midpoint reversal changes the protagonist's understanding of the conflict.
The dark night of the soul is the low point before the climax.
Climax: the protagonist faces their greatest fear and makes the defining choice.
Denouement resolves remaining threads and shows the new normal.
Chekhov's gun: every element introduced must serve a purpose.
Foreshadowing creates subconscious expectations that pay off later.
Unreliable narrators force readers to question everything they've been told.
In medias res: starting in the middle of action hooks the reader immediately.
The hero's journey (Campbell) maps transformation through 12 stages.
Save the Cat: the protagonist does something likable early to earn reader investment.
Pacing varies sentence length — short sentences for action, long for reflection.
Setting is not backdrop; it shapes character behavior and constrains plot.
Theme emerges from the story — never preach it directly.
Revision is where the real writing happens — first drafts are for discovery.
Kill your darlings: remove beautiful passages that don't serve the story.
Every scene must change something — if the status quo is the same after, cut it.
